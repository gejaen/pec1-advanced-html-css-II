The Fernsehturm (English: Television Tower) in central Berlin was constructed between 1965 and 1969 by the government of the German Democratic Republic, or East Germany, as both a functional broadcasting facility and a symbol of Communist power. It remains a landmark today from its position next to Alexanderplatz in the city's Marien Quarter, part of the district of Mitte, visible across most suburban districts of Berlin.[1] With its height of 368 metres (including antenna) it is the tallest structure in Germany, and the third-tallest structure in the European Union. When built it was the fourth-tallest freestanding structure in the world after the Ostankino Tower, the Empire State Building and 875 North Michigan Avenue, then known as The John Hancock Center.

Some facts

1960

With the advent of television broadcasting, there was a need for a television tower in Berlin to ensure uninterrupted transmission of TV signals. In 1965, the construction of the Fernsehturm Berlin began.

1969

The Fernsehturm Berlin was completed after four years of construction and opened to the public on October 3. At a height of 368 meters, it was the tallest structure in Germany at the time and the second-tallest in Europe.

1970s

The tower became a popular tourist attraction, with visitors from all over the world coming to enjoy panoramic views of Berlin from the observation deck. In the 1970s, a revolving restaurant was added to the tower's structure, providing visitors with an elegant dining experience.

1980s

In 1984, the Fernsehturm Berlin was declared a historic landmark by the East German government. Throughout the decade, the tower remained a symbol of East Germany's technological prowess and modernity, as well as a source of national pride.

1990

In the years following German reunification, the Fernsehturm Berlin underwent extensive renovations to modernize its equipment and improve its safety standards. The observation deck and revolving restaurant were also updated to provide visitors with a better experience.

1991

In 1991, a lightning strike damaged the tower's antenna, leading to a temporary closure for repairs. The tower reopened soon after, and since then, it has remained an important landmark of the city and a popular tourist attraction.

2000s

In 2005, the tower underwent another major renovation to modernize the interior and exterior of the structure. During this time, the tower was closed to the public for several months. The renovations included the installation of new elevators, updated lighting systems, and a new entrance area.

2010s

In 2011, a new viewing platform called the "Panorama Floor" was opened at a height of 203 meters, offering visitors an even more impressive panoramic view of the city. In 2016, the tower celebrated its 50th anniversary with a special light show and other events.

2020

In March 2020, the Fernsehturm Berlin was temporarily closed to the public due to the COVID-19 pandemic. It reopened in June 2020 with reduced capacity and safety measures in place, including mandatory mask-wearing and social distancing guidelines. The tower continues to be an important landmark of the city and a popular tourist attraction, offering stunning views of Berlin from its observation deck and revolving restaurant.
